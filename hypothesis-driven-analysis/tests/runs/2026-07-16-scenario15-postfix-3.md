# Investigation: Did the Assist workflow cause faster incident recovery in the pilot, and how many responder-hours can we credibly attribute to it?

## Problem

- Decision informed: whether Monday's operations review recommends expanding Assist company-wide, and whether Finance books a responder-hours savings figure into the rollout plan.
- Falsifiable question: for incidents opened June 1-14 in the identity and storage service groups, did the June 8 00:00 UTC cutover to the Assist workflow cause a reduction in time-to-close and/or responder-minutes, net of any confounds that moved at the same time?
- Success criteria: answered means we can state, with named evidence, whether the observed before/after change is attributable to Assist versus identified confounds, and either produce a defensible responder-hours estimate with its basis or state explicitly why the data cannot support one.
- Stop condition: conclude once (a) the naive aggregate contrast, (b) a severity-stratified/reweighted contrast, (c) a censoring-adjusted contrast, and (d) a difficulty-interaction check on the hardest incidents have all been run; iterate only if a specific further test could still reverse the answer within budget.
- Effort budget: ~20 analysis steps (python/bash queries over the three local CSVs).

## Plan orientation (pre-registration findings; no cause-outcome relationship inspected)

- 420 incidents, June 1-14, exactly 15/day/group, 210 identity / 210 storage, split exactly 210 manual / 210 assist along the June 8 00:00 UTC cutover.
- 409/420 incidents have a matching activity (closure) row; 11 have none (no `closed_at`) at extract time.
- Of those 11 unresolved incidents, 10 belong to the `assist` period and only 1 to `manual`; 9 of the 10 assist-period ones are identity; 5 of the 11 are sev1.
- Severity mix differs sharply by period: manual = 13.3% sev1 / 40.0% sev2 / 46.7% sev3; assist = 6.7% sev1 / 20.0% sev2 / 73.3% sev3. Intake-channel mix and service-group split are balanced across periods.
- `staffing.csv`: active_responders and scheduled_responder_hours step up for both service groups on exactly June 8 (identity 12→14, storage 11→13) — the same date as the Assist cutover, with no within-period variation in staffing. interruption_minutes also rises sharply after June 8 (roughly 5-44/day before, 13-110/day after).
- No independent denominator (e.g., a separate ticketing total) exists to validate incident-count coverage; the exact 15/day/group regularity is internally consistent but unverifiable against an outside source.

## Data Validity

- Collection method: three frozen local CSV exports pulled 23:59 UTC June 22; incidents.csv logs every incident opened June 1-14 with the workflow active at open time; activity.csv logs resolution activity as of the extract; staffing.csv is a daily operating snapshot per service group.
- Coverage matrix (date × service_group × workflow, incidents opened vs. matched activity rows): uniform 15 incidents/day/group across all 14 days (420 total, 210/210 manual-assist, 210/210 identity-storage — no gaps). Matched-activity shortfall is concentrated entirely in the assist period: 1 missing in manual (2026-06-05, identity), vs. 10 missing in assist, spread across 2026-06-08, 06-10, 06-11, 06-12, 06-13, 06-14, mostly identity (9 of 10).
- Field population: `closed_at`/`responder_minutes`/`handoffs` are 97.4% populated overall (409/420) but the 2.6% gap is not random — it is 10x more likely in the assist period than the manual period and skews toward sev1 (5 of 14 assist sev1 incidents, 35.7%, are unresolved at extract vs. 0 of 28 manual sev1 incidents). `severity`, `service_group`, `intake_channel`, `workflow`, `opened_at` are 100% populated in S1.
- Coverage baseline: no independent denominator (e.g. a separate ticketing system count) exists to confirm the 15/day/group regularity is genuine and not itself an artifact of the extract; recorded as unverifiable rather than clean. The incidents↔activity join itself is used as an internal cross-check (see above) and is sufficient to detect the censoring pattern even without an external baseline.
- Known instrument failures: right-censoring at the June 22 23:59 UTC extract boundary — incidents opened June 8-14 have had 8-14 days to close, but any incident still open at extract time is absent from activity.csv entirely rather than flagged as open, so a naive time-to-close computation silently drops it.
- Sensitivity checks performed: the censoring pattern (10/210 assist vs 1/210 manual unresolved) is large relative to the overall 2.6% missing rate and concentrated in exactly the severity band (sev1) most likely to drive median time-to-close, so this is not a detection-limit-bound null result — it is a directional, severity-skewed asymmetry visible at raw counts.

## Conclusion

- **Answer**: The pilot's "Assist is faster" reading is not supported once the comparison is made fair. At matched severity, Assist incidents took longer and consumed more responder-minutes than manual-workflow incidents in every stratum (sev1, sev2, sev3) — the opposite of the dashboard's headline. The dashboard's median-time-to-close improvement is fully explained by two things that moved at the same time as the workflow: (1) the mix of incidents opened during the Assist week shifted heavily toward easy sev3 tickets (73.3% vs 46.7% before) and away from hard sev1 tickets (6.7% vs 13.3% before), which mechanically pulls a median down even with zero per-incident change; and (2) right-censoring — the hardest Assist-week incidents (5 of 14 sev1 tickets, 35.7%) are still open 8.6–15 days after the extract and are silently excluded from "time to close," while manual-week sev1 had zero such dropouts. On top of this, every sev1 incident that *did* close under Assist reopened within 72 hours (9/9 = 100%, vs 0% everywhere else in the dataset) — a pattern that looks like premature closes on exactly the incidents responders describe as "genuinely difficult." Separately, staffing increased for both service groups on the exact same day as the Assist cutover with zero independent variation, so this dataset cannot separate "Assist helped/hurt" from "more staff helped/hurt" as causal stories — but it does mean the worse matched-severity outcomes occurred *despite* more staff being on hand, not because of a staffing shortfall.
- **Best supported**: H3 (severity-mix composition explains the aggregate median movement — T2 reweighting more than fully reproduces it) and H4/H5 together (censoring and a sev1-specific reopen/burden pattern — T3, T4), both discriminating against H1 because they explain the same observations without requiring Assist to have helped anyone. H1 (Assist causally helps) has no supporting evidence anywhere in the data and is contradicted in every severity stratum tested.
- Per-hypothesis summary:

  | id | claim | status | basis |
  | --- | --- | --- | --- |
  | H1 | causal | UNRESOLVED | necessary prediction (assist ≤ manual within severity strata) failed in T2 for every stratum and worsened further under censoring-adjustment in T3; because the design is observational with no holdout (confirmed unidentifiable per H2/T5), this cannot be marked `REFUTED`, but there is no positive evidence for it anywhere in the data, and it is not best-supported |
  | H2 | causal | UNRESOLVED | `NON_DISCRIMINATING` (T5) — staffing changed in perfect lockstep with the workflow cutover for both service groups, zero within-period variation, so this dataset cannot test a staffing-alone explanation at all |
  | H3 | descriptive (estimand: share of the raw aggregate time-to-close/responder-minutes gap explained by the severity-mix shift between periods) | UNRESOLVED | best supported — T2's reweighting fully reproduces (and reverses the sign of) the raw aggregate gap, showing composition, not per-incident speed, drives the headline number |
  | H4 | data-artifact | UNRESOLVED | best supported — T3 confirms an open-incident rate 10x higher in the assist period, severity-skewed toward sev1 (35.7% vs 0%), and a censoring-aware lower bound reverses the naive "assist is faster" reading |
  | H5 | causal | UNRESOLVED | best supported — T2 (higher resp-min/ttc within every stratum), T3 (sev1 unresolved-rate asymmetry), and T4 (100% reopen rate isolated to sev1×assist, absent everywhere else) converge; associative only, no identified design |
- **Limitations**: This is an observational before/after comparison with a single simultaneous cutover for both pilot groups — no holdout, no randomization, and per T5 the staffing increase is perfectly collinear with the Assist cutover, so *no* test on this data can issue a clean causal verdict on Assist alone, in either direction. The 5 still-open sev1 Assist incidents have unknown eventual duration; T3's lower bound almost certainly understates their true cost, so the true sev1-Assist penalty is at least as large as reported, likely larger. The 100% sev1-Assist reopen rate rests on n=9 closed incidents — small, but categorically clean (0% everywhere else in 409 rows), which is why it clears the discriminating bar despite the small count. No independent denominator exists to validate incident-count coverage (Data Validity), though this does not bear on the direction of the findings above. All causal language here is associative per the skill's rules — treat "Assist is associated with worse matched-severity outcomes" as the ceiling of what this data can say, not proof Assist caused the degradation; a concurrent staffing change, interruption-rate increase, or an unmeasured factor tied to the same calendar week could also be involved.

# Decision Memo — Assist Workflow Expansion (for Monday's Operations Review)

**Question**: Should we expand the Assist workflow to the rest of the company, and how many responder-hours can Finance credibly book as savings?

**Headline**: No — the evidence does not support expansion, and no responder-hours savings should be booked. The dashboard's "median time to close fell" is real as a raw statistic but is an artifact of two things that moved with the cutover, not evidence Assist works: the incident mix shifted toward easier tickets during the Assist week, and the hardest Assist-week tickets are disproportionately still open and invisible to the median. Once the comparison is made apples-to-apples (same severity, accounting for still-open incidents), Assist incidents took longer and cost more responder-minutes than manual-workflow incidents in every severity band tested.

**What the data shows**:
1. **Severity-matched comparison reverses the headline.** Holding severity fixed, Assist incidents were slower and more expensive in every band: sev1 median time-to-close 1100→1659 minutes, sev2 512.5→740, sev3 167→302 (manual→assist). Responder-minutes moved the same way in every band.
2. **The aggregate "improvement" is a composition effect.** The Assist week's incident mix was 73.3% low-severity (sev3) vs 46.7% before, and only 6.7% sev1 vs 13.3% before. Reweighting manual outcomes to the Assist week's severity mix reproduces — and exceeds — the entire observed aggregate gap. The "faster" median is measuring a change in what kind of incidents came in that week, not what the workflow did to any given incident.
3. **The hardest incidents are disappearing from the measurement, not resolving faster.** 35.7% of sev1 Assist incidents (5 of 14) are still open 9–15 days after the extract and are simply absent from the "time to close" calculation — versus 0% for sev1 manual incidents. Counting them at their current elapsed time (a conservative floor, not their true eventual cost) flips the sev1 comparison decisively against Assist.
4. **A specific quality signal on hard incidents.** Every sev1 incident that did close under Assist (9 of 9) reopened within 72 hours. That is a unique pattern — 0% everywhere else across 409 incidents — consistent with responders' reports that Assist creates extra work on genuinely difficult incidents, possibly via premature or incomplete closes.
5. **The pilot cannot isolate Assist's effect from a concurrent staffing increase.** Both pilot groups' headcount and scheduled hours rose on the exact same day as the Assist cutover, with no independent variation anywhere in the 14-day window. No analysis of this data — including this one — can cleanly separate "Assist changed outcomes" from "more staff changed outcomes." That said, outcomes at matched severity got *worse* even with more staff on hand, which does not favor an optimistic reading of Assist's effect.

**Responder-hours for Finance**: Do not book a savings figure. Finance's headline number comes from an unadjusted median that this analysis shows is driven by severity-mix composition and censoring, not a real per-incident effect. If forced to give a point estimate from the same data, the severity-adjusted comparison points the opposite direction — roughly **90+ responder-hours more consumed**, not saved, across the two-week pilot (≈26 extra minutes/incident × 210 assist-period incidents), and that is a floor: it excludes the unknown eventual cost of the 5 still-open sev1 incidents. This number is associative, not causal — the staffing confound in point 5 means it cannot be attributed to Assist with confidence either. The right number to give Finance today is **no defensible savings figure, and a plausible net cost**, not a credit.

**Recommendation**: Do not expand Assist company-wide on the current evidence.
- Do not book responder-hours savings in the rollout plan.
- Treat the sev1 100%-reopen pattern as a quality defect to investigate before any further rollout — find out what "closed" means for those 9 incidents.
- Resolve the 5 still-open sev1 Assist incidents and re-run this analysis once they mature past censoring.
- If leadership still wants to pursue expansion, redesign the rollout to separate the staffing change from the workflow change (stagger them, or hold staffing constant) and use a holdout or staggered group so the next read isn't confounded the same way this pilot was.
