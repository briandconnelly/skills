# Investigation: Did enabling Assist cause faster incident resolution in the pilot groups, and how many responder-hours can be credibly attributed to it?

## Problem

- Decision informed: whether to recommend expanding Assist company-wide at Monday's ops review, and what (if any) responder-hours figure to hand Finance for the rollout plan.
- Falsifiable question: did switching `identity` and `storage` from the manual workflow to Assist at 2026-06-08T00:00Z cause a reduction in incident time-to-close and/or responder-minutes, net of any severity-mix, staffing, and censoring differences between the manual week (Jun 1-7) and the assist week (Jun 8-14)?
- Success criteria: answered means, for both time-to-close and responder-minutes, we can state whether the change is attributable to Assist, better explained by a confound/artifact, or not identifiable from this data — plus either a defensible responder-hours figure or an explicit statement that none is defensible — and whether the responders' "extra work on difficult incidents" complaint is corroborated or not.
- Stop condition: conclude once severity-mix, staffing, and censoring confounds have been checked against both primary outcomes and no further check available in these three files could change the recommendation.
- Effort budget: 25 analysis queries over the local exports (no re-collection; the extract is frozen, not metered). Used: ~9.

## Assignment mechanism (routing note)

Both pilot groups were switched to Assist in a single cutover at 2026-06-08T00:00Z; there was no holdout, no randomization, and no stated independence between assignment and the outcome — every incident's workflow is fully determined by when it opened. This is the "launched it, shipped to everyone, happened in a week when other things also happened" case the skill routes to **full**: nothing identifies the causal effect, and every co-occurring change (composition, staffing, censoring) is a live rival explanation, not noise to average away. The service owners' claim that the comparison is "clean" because every incident followed the workflow active when it opened describes consistent labeling, not identification — it says nothing about whether the two weeks are otherwise comparable.

## Data Validity

- Collection method: three flat CSV exports pulled at a single point in time (2026-06-22T23:59Z); no streaming/live feed, so all fields reflect state as of that instant.
- Coverage matrix (workflow x service_group x severity, incident counts and closed-row completeness from S1/S2):
  - manual: identity/sev1 14 (100% closed), identity/sev2 42 (100%), identity/sev3 49 (98.0%); storage/sev1 14 (100%), storage/sev2 42 (100%), storage/sev3 49 (100%)
  - assist: identity/sev1 7 (57.1% closed), identity/sev2 21 (90.5%), identity/sev3 77 (97.4%); storage/sev1 7 (71.4%), storage/sev2 21 (100%), storage/sev3 77 (98.7%)
  - The manual week is essentially fully matured (>=98% closed in every cell). The assist week has a severe, severity-concentrated shortfall: under 60% closed for identity/sev1, the smallest and hardest cell, and 71% for storage/sev1.
- Field population: `closed_at`, `responder_minutes`, `handoffs`, `reopened_within_72h` are 100% populated for every row present in activity.csv — there is no null problem, only a row-absence (censoring) problem, which the coverage matrix above (not a null count) is what surfaces.
- Coverage baseline: no independent "incidents still open" counter exists in these exports; the baseline used is the internal one available — the set difference between incidents.csv ids and activity.csv ids, cross-checked against each open incident's `opened_at` vs the extract timestamp (elapsed days so far). Recorded as the best available check, not an independent-system baseline.
- Known instrument failures: none documented; the shortfall above is a real censoring pattern (asymmetric maturation window — assist incidents opened Jun 8-14 had 8-14 days to close by the Jun 22 extract, vs 15-21 days for manual incidents), not a logging gap. The prompt's framing ("even the newest incidents had at least seven full days to mature") is true but insufficient: 7 days is not enough for the slowest cases, as T3 shows.
- Sensitivity checks performed: the same workflow x field crosstab method applied to `intake_channel` (a field not expected to shift with the cutover) shows a roughly stable mix (customer ~29-31%, monitor ~34-38%, partner ~33-36% in both weeks) — confirming the method is not spuriously finding imbalance everywhere, which makes the severity-mix imbalance found for H2 (sev1 13.3%->6.7%, sev3 46.7%->73.3%) a credible signal rather than a detection artifact. Day-of-week composition is identical between the two weeks (both run Mon-Sun), ruling out weekday/weekend mix as a further confound.

## Conclusion

- **Answer:** The headline ("median time to close fell in the Assist week") is real as a marginal, unadjusted number, but it is not evidence Assist caused faster recovery. It is fully explained by two co-occurring, non-causal factors: (1) the assist week's incident mix shifted heavily toward low-severity, fast-closing incidents (sev1 share roughly halved, sev3 share rose from 47% to 73%), and (2) the assist week's hardest incidents are disproportionately still open at extract time and were dropped from the "closed incidents" median entirely (43-29% of assist/sev1 incidents remain open after 8-15 days, versus a fully matured manual week). Once severity is held constant, assist incidents took longer and consumed more responder-minutes than manual incidents in **every** severity stratum (+44% to +81% slower on time-to-close, +29% to +67% more responder-minutes), and a staffing increase of similar magnitude (+17-19%) started on the exact same day as the workflow change, so even this within-stratum association cannot be attributed to Assist specifically — it is confounded the same way, just in the other direction. Nothing in this dataset identifies Assist's causal effect either way.
- **Best supported:** H2 (severity-mix composition explains the marginal "faster" reading — T1, standardized comparison fully reverses the sign) and H4 (censoring is material and severity-concentrated — T3, elapsed time and severity skew both confirm it). H5 (responders' "extra work on difficult incidents" complaint) is also best supported (T4, responder-minutes higher under assist in every stratum, corroborated by the reopen-rate pattern on sev1). H1 (Assist causally helps) is not supported by any test and is contradicted on every within-stratum comparison run, but per the skill's identification rule an unidentified exposure-outcome contrast cannot mark a causal hypothesis `REFUTED` on its own — it stays `UNRESOLVED`, with the evidence weighing against it. H3 (staffing as alternative driver) is structurally untestable from this data (perfect collinearity with the workflow cutover) and stays `UNRESOLVED`.
- Per-hypothesis summary:

  | id | claim | status | basis |
  | --- | --- | --- | --- |
  | H1 | causal | UNRESOLVED | necessary prediction (assist faster within-stratum) failed under T1 on every stratum, but the contrast is not causally identified (no holdout, single cutover), so per the skill's rule this cannot mark it REFUTED — evidence weighs against it but a masking co-exposure cannot be ruled out |
  | H2 | descriptive | UNRESOLVED (best supported) | T1 CONSISTENT — severity-standardized comparison fully reverses the marginal gap, discriminating it from H1 |
  | H3 | causal | UNRESOLVED | T2 NON_DISCRIMINATING — staffing changed in perfect lockstep with workflow, zero within-week independent variation to test on |
  | H4 | data-artifact | UNRESOLVED (best supported) | T3 CONSISTENT — censored incidents' elapsed time and severity skew both confirm a material, severity-concentrated censoring bias in the closed-incident sample the dashboard's median was built from |
  | H5 | descriptive | UNRESOLVED (best supported) | T4 CONSISTENT — responder-minutes higher under assist in every severity stratum; corroborated, not contradicted, by the sev1 reopen-rate pattern |

- Limitations:
  - No holdout and no randomization: the manual-vs-assist contrast is confounded with calendar time itself, so severity mix, staffing, and any other week-over-week change (seasonality, on-call rotation, upstream dependency incidents, etc.) cannot be ruled out even after the checks above — this analysis can rule *in* two concrete confounds (severity mix, censoring) and identify a third as untestable (staffing); it cannot rule out unknown ones.
  - The within-stratum comparison itself is not causally clean either, for the same reason the marginal comparison isn't: it is still one week vs. another week, just matched on one covariate (severity). It is a stronger, more skeptical check than the marginal number, not a causal estimate.
  - Assist/sev1 in particular is data-thin (9 closed of 14 incidents-worth-of-equivalent-volume, with the rest still open) and its "0 handoffs" figure is likely an artifact of premature closure (100% 72h-reopen rate on that n=9) rather than genuinely light handling — treat any assist/sev1-specific number as provisional pending those incidents actually closing.
  - This analysis covers only the two pilot groups (identity, storage) over two specific weeks; it says nothing directly about how Assist would perform in other service groups with different incident mixes or staffing models.
  - All claims here are associative. No design in this dataset supports counterfactual ("what would have happened without Assist") language for either direction.

## Decision Memo — Assist Rollout, Monday Ops Review

**To:** Operations Review
**Re:** Should we expand Assist company-wide, and what responder-hours figure goes in the rollout plan?

**Bottom line:** Do not expand yet, and do not book responder-hour savings from the pilot. The "faster in the Assist week" finding the dashboard reported is real as a raw number but is fully explained by two things that have nothing to do with Assist: the Assist week happened to get a much easier mix of incidents (far fewer sev1/sev2, far more sev3), and the hardest Assist-week incidents are disproportionately still unresolved and were silently excluded from the median. When the comparison is held constant for severity, Assist incidents took longer and used more responder time than manual incidents at every severity level — the opposite of the headline. A staffing increase of similar size started on the same day as the rollout, which further muddies any read in either direction. There was no holdout group, so none of this — in either direction — can be called a causal effect of Assist; it's association only, and it currently points against the "it's helping" story rather than for it.

**Why the headline is misleading:**
- Marginal median time-to-close: 436 min (manual) -> 326.5 min (assist), a ~25% improvement — this is the number behind the dashboard and Finance's hours-saved figure.
- Same comparison, held constant for severity: assist was 44-81% *slower* in every severity band (sev1: 1100 -> 1659 min; sev2: 512.5 -> 740 min; sev3: 167 -> 302 min).
- Reweighting assist's own within-severity results to the manual week's severity mix gives a standardized median of 658 minutes for assist — worse than manual's 436, not better. The improvement is entirely a byproduct of the assist week having easier incidents, not faster handling of the same incidents.
- Separately, 29-43% of the assist week's sev1 incidents are still open 8-15 days after the extract, versus a fully closed-out manual week; those unresolved, presumably hardest cases are the ones missing from the "median time to close" the dashboard reported.

**Responder-hours for Finance:** Give Finance **0 hours of credible, Assist-attributable savings.** The naive number behind their current line item (~60 responder-hours "saved" across the two-group pilot week) is a composition/censoring artifact, not a measured effect. If anything, the like-for-like (severity-matched) association points toward *more* responder time per incident under Assist, but that number (roughly 130 hours, at assist volume) is equally non-causal — driven by the same lack of a holdout, and confounded further by an 18-19% staffing increase that started the same day — so it should not be booked as a cost either. The honest position is that this dataset cannot support any responder-hours line item for or against Assist.

**On the responders' complaint:** The data corroborates it. Responder-minutes were higher under Assist than under manual for every severity band, and handoffs were higher for sev2/sev3. The one place handoffs look better under Assist (sev1) is undermined by a 100% 72-hour-reopen rate on that small sample, suggesting those were premature closures, not genuinely easier resolutions.

**Recommendation:** Hold expansion. Before a company-wide rollout decision, run a proper test — a staggered or randomized rollout with a concurrent holdout, or at minimum a design that doesn't move workflow and staffing on the same day — so the comparison isn't hopelessly confounded. In parallel, look directly at what's happening on Assist's sev1 cases in the pilot: the open-incident backlog and the reopen pattern are a concrete, near-term problem independent of the causal question, and are worth investigating regardless of what a future controlled test finds.
